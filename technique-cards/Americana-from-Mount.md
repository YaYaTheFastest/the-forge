# Americana from Mount

## 1. Core Technique

**Name:** Americana (Keylock) from Mount  
**Belt Level:** Blue belt (fundamental submission, introduced at white belt in many curricula)  
**Position/Entry:** Top mount control, typically after establishing high mount or following a failed armbar attempt. Can be entered from side control as a transition into mount or directly when opponent frames with elbow on mat.

## 2. Key Principles

- **Leverage:** Use the figure-four grip to create a mechanical advantage that isolates the opponent's shoulder joint against their own arm as a fulcrum. Your chest and body weight provide the finishing pressure rather than arm strength.
- **Angles:** Keep your chest square or slightly angled toward the trapped arm. The opponent's elbow must be pinned below 90 degrees relative to their torso; lifting the elbow too high reduces effectiveness.
- **Timing:** Best applied when opponent is defending with one arm posted on the mat or framing against your hip/neck. Capitalize immediately after they extend an arm to push or frame.
- **Common Mistakes:** 
  - Allowing the opponent to roll to their side (must block hip escape with knee pressure).
  - Losing the figure-four grip by letting the wrist slip.
  - Applying pressure upward instead of across the body toward the opponent's head.
  - Neglecting to control the head or far arm, allowing them to create space.

## 3. Step-by-Step Execution

1. Establish and maintain strong mount position with knees high and tight to their ribs/hips. Your weight centered low.
2. Identify the target arm (usually the one they use to frame). Pin their wrist to the mat with your same-side hand, keeping their elbow bent and elbow below shoulder level.
3. Thread your opposite hand under their trapped arm and grab your own wrist (figure-four / Kimura-style grip but reversed direction).
4. Walk your knees up toward their head to "high mount" while keeping their wrist pinned. This removes their ability to roll or shrimp effectively.
5. Rotate your torso slightly toward the trapped side and drive your elbows toward the mat while pulling their wrist upward toward their head. Apply steady, increasing pressure across their body.
6. Finish by pinching your elbows together and leaning your chest weight forward. Look for the tap as shoulder external rotation reaches its limit.
7. **Personal cue:** "Elbow to ear" — drive their hand toward their own ear while keeping their elbow glued to the floor. Use "heavy hips, light arms" to avoid muscling.

## 4. Variations & Transitions

- **High Mount Americana:** Walked up even higher for more leverage when opponent bridges hard.
- **Americana from Side Control:** Enter before mounting; useful when mount is unstable.
- **Reverse Americana (Kimura-style from mount):** When opponent hides the arm differently.
- **Transition to Armbar:** If they roll hard to escape Americana, switch to armbar by releasing the figure-four and swinging the leg over.
- **To S-Mount or Technical Mount:** Use the threat of Americana to force them to give up back exposure or other submissions.
- **Counter to Bridge & Roll:** When they attempt to roll you, use the momentum to spin into technical mount while maintaining the lock.
